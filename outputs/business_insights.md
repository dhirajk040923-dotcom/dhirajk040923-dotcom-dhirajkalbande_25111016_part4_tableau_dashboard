# Business Insights — Retail Executive Dashboard

**Dataset:** dashboard_sales_data.xlsx | **Total Records:** 4,200 orders | **Period:** 2024–2025

---

## Insight 1: Steady Revenue Growth But Thin Profit Expansion (Sales Trend)

**Observation:**
Total sales grew from approximately $106.2M in 2024 to $110.8M in 2025 — a 4.3% year-over-year increase. However, profit grew only marginally, from $16.47M to $16.83M (2.2% growth), meaning profit growth lagged behind revenue growth.

**Data Evidence:**
- 2024 Sales: $106,241,000 | Profit: $16,472,754
- 2025 Sales: $110,776,600 | Profit: $16,833,559
- Overall Profit Margin: 15.35%
- Peak sales months: August 2025 ($10.86M), July 2025 ($10.66M), September 2024 ($10.49M)

**Business Interpretation:**
While the business is growing, profit is not scaling at the same rate as revenue. This suggests rising costs, increased discounting, or a shift in product mix toward lower-margin items. The mid-year sales peaks (July–September) indicate a strong summer demand cycle that should be planned for deliberately.

**Recommended Action:**
Investigate cost structures and discount policies between 2024 and 2025. Run a promotion-efficiency review to determine whether discounts offered during peak months are cannibalising margin. Prioritise high-margin product lines during peak periods.

## Insight 2: South Region Leads in Sales, But All Regions Are Balanced (Regional Performance)

**Observation:**
The South region generates the highest sales ($64.7M, 29.8% of total) and profit ($9.99M), followed by North ($54.6M), West ($48.9M), and East ($48.9M). No region is significantly underperforming, but the South holds a clear lead.

**Data Evidence:**
| Region | Sales | Profit | Profit Margin |
|--------|-------|--------|---------------|
| South  | $64.7M | $9.99M | 15.4% |
| North  | $54.6M | $8.31M | 15.2% |
| West   | $48.9M | $7.40M | 15.1% |
| East   | $48.9M | $7.60M | 15.5% |

**Business Interpretation:**
Profit margins are nearly identical across all regions (15.1–15.5%), which suggests pricing and cost structures are consistent nationally. The South's higher sales volume may reflect a larger customer base or more active sales force rather than better pricing strategy.

**Recommended Action:**
Investigate what drives the South's higher volume — is it population density, more sales reps, or specific product demand? Replicate those drivers in the East and West, which have near-identical sales figures and clear room to grow.

## Insight 3: Technology Dominates Profit — Furniture and Office Supplies Are Underperformers (Category Profitability)

**Observation:**
Technology generates $153.9M in sales (70.9% of total revenue) and $28.04M in profit (84.2% of total profit). Furniture and Office Supplies together account for $63.1M in sales but only $5.26M in profit combined — just 15.8% of total profit.

**Data Evidence:**
| Category | Sales | Profit | Margin |
|----------|-------|--------|--------|
| Technology | $153.9M | $28.04M | 18.2% |
| Furniture | $51.6M | $3.56M | 6.9% |
| Office Supplies | $11.5M | $1.71M | 14.9% |

At sub-category level, Copiers ($7.31M profit), Accessories ($7.19M), and Phones ($7.10M) are the top three profit drivers. Bookcases ($711K), Tables ($731K), and Paper ($319K) sit at the bottom.

**Business Interpretation:**
The business is heavily dependent on Technology products for profitability. Furniture's 6.9% margin is dangerously thin and a risk if sales volumes dip. Office Supplies has acceptable margins but very low total sales volume.

**Recommended Action:**
Review the Furniture pricing strategy — particularly Bookcases and Tables which have high sales volume but very low profit. Consider reducing promotions on already low-margin furniture items and redirecting marketing spend toward Technology accessories and copiers where margin is strongest.

## Insight 4: Home Office Segment Has Highest Sales and Return Risk (Customer Segment Behaviour)

**Observation:**
Home Office customers generate the highest revenue ($74.5M) and profit ($11.56M), but also have the highest return rate at 6%, compared to 4% for both Consumer and Corporate segments.

**Data Evidence:**
| Segment | Sales | Profit | Return Rate |
|---------|-------|--------|-------------|
| Home Office | $74.5M | $11.56M | 6.0% |
| Consumer | $71.9M | $11.03M | 4.0% |
| Corporate | $70.6M | $10.72M | 4.0% |

**Business Interpretation:**
Home Office is the most valuable segment by revenue, but its elevated return rate signals a product-fit or expectation-mismatch issue. Returns directly erode profit margin — every returned order incurs reverse logistics costs on top of the lost sale.

**Recommended Action:**
Conduct a return-reason analysis specifically for Home Office customers. Review whether product descriptions, packaging, or size/compatibility information needs improvement. A 2% return rate reduction in this segment could recover significant margin.

## Insight 5: High Discounts Are Destroying Profit (Discount Impact)

**Observation:**
As discount levels increase, profit drops sharply. Orders with no discount generate a profit margin of approximately 20.6%, while orders with 21–50% discounts generate only 7.5% margin. There are no orders with discounts above 50%, which is a positive guardrail.

**Data Evidence:**
| Discount Level | Sales | Profit | Implied Margin |
|---------------|-------|--------|----------------|
| No discount | $65.8M | $13.52M | 20.6% |
| 1–10% | $57.6M | $9.80M | 17.0% |
| 11–20% | $48.7M | $6.63M | 13.6% |
| 21–50% | $45.0M | $3.35M | 7.4% |

**Business Interpretation:**
There is a clear, direct inverse relationship between discount depth and profitability. Orders in the 21–50% discount bracket generate less than a third of the profit per sale compared to undiscounted orders, yet still represent $45M in revenue — meaning a large portion of the business is effectively subsidising customer purchases.

**Recommended Action:**
Set a company-wide discount cap of 20%. Require management approval for any discount above 15%. Introduce a discount-effectiveness review: test whether discounts in the 11–20% range are actually driving incremental volume or simply reducing price for customers who would have bought anyway.

## Insight 6: Standard Class Shipping Carries Most Revenue But Has Longest Delays (Shipping & Delivery Performance)

**Observation:**
Standard Class handles 56.6% of all sales ($122.8M) with an average delivery time of 4.71 days. Same Day shipping is the fastest (0.40 days average) but accounts for only 6.7% of sales. First Class offers a good middle ground at 1.77 days average delivery.

**Data Evidence:**
| Ship Mode | Sales | Profit | Avg Delivery Days |
|-----------|-------|--------|-------------------|
| Standard Class | $122.8M | $19.09M | 4.71 days |
| Second Class | $47.7M | $7.39M | 2.68 days |
| First Class | $31.9M | $4.64M | 1.77 days |
| Same Day | $14.6M | $2.19M | 0.40 days |

**Business Interpretation:**
The majority of the business runs on Standard Class, which at nearly 5 days average delivery may be creating customer satisfaction issues — particularly for Home Office customers who have the highest return rates. Faster shipping options are available but underutilised.

**Recommended Action:**
Survey customers on delivery satisfaction specifically for Standard Class orders. Consider offering free upgrades to Second Class for high-value orders above a threshold (e.g. $500+) as a retention tool, particularly for the Home Office segment. Monitor whether delivery speed correlates with return rates.

## Insight 7: Furniture Has Alarmingly High Return Rate — Nearly Twice the Average (Return Pattern)

**Observation:**
Furniture has a return rate of 7.67%, which is more than double the Technology return rate (3.03%) and significantly above Office Supplies (3.65%). The overall return rate across the business is 4.55%.

**Data Evidence:**
| Category | Return Rate |
|----------|-------------|
| Furniture | 7.67% |
| Office Supplies | 3.65% |
| Technology | 3.03% |
| **Overall** | **4.55%** |

**Business Interpretation:**
Furniture's high return rate combined with its already thin 6.9% profit margin creates a double risk: low margins being further eroded by return processing costs. A single furniture return can wipe out the profit from 2–3 other furniture sales. This is the highest-risk category in the portfolio.

**Recommended Action:**
Immediately investigate the root causes of furniture returns — is it damage in transit, wrong dimensions, assembly issues, or product quality? Implement improved packaging standards for large furniture items. Consider adding 360-degree product views and detailed dimension guides online to reduce mismatch returns. Review whether delivery partners handling furniture have higher damage rates.

## Insight 8: Organic Channel Drives 40% of Revenue — Paid Channels Are Underperforming Relative to Investment (Business Risk / Opportunity)

**Observation:**
Organic traffic generates $88.8M in sales (40.9% of total) and $13.44M in profit. Email and Paid channels together contribute $74.6M in sales. Referral, despite being low-cost, generates $21.2M — a strong return for its likely lower acquisition cost.

**Data Evidence:**
| Channel | Sales | Profit | Profit Margin |
|---------|-------|--------|---------------|
| Organic | $88.8M | $13.44M | 15.1% |
| Paid | $40.1M | $6.05M | 15.1% |
| Email | $34.5M | $5.36M | 15.5% |
| Social | $31.1M | $4.91M | 15.8% |
| Referral | $21.2M | $3.34M | 15.7% |

**Business Interpretation:**
All channels produce similar profit margins (15.1–15.8%), which means the question is not about which channel is more efficient per sale, but which channel is most cost-effective to acquire customers through. Organic is dominant but inherently harder to scale. Referral has strong margin and is typically low-cost — making it a high-opportunity underinvested channel. Social has the highest margin (15.8%) and may be underutilised.

**Recommended Action:**
Invest in a structured referral programme (e.g. refer-a-friend incentives) to scale the Referral channel, which currently delivers strong returns at likely lower acquisition cost. Audit Paid channel spend — if cost-per-acquisition is high, reallocate budget toward Social and Email which have slightly better margins. Build an SEO and content strategy to protect and grow the Organic channel, which is the single largest revenue driver.
